New Interviews

First one did not want to give us a name, I’m just gonna call him “Bobby”.
Second one is Victoria

1. Have you ever used any form of artificial intelligence (i.e. ChatGPT, Deepseek, Copilot, etc.)? If so, how often do you use it, and for what purposes?

Bobby: Yes

Victoria: I've used it in the past to check to see if strange student responses used it and thus needed to be reported for an honor code violation.

2. How important do you think AI will be in the future and why?

Bobby: Moderately important; generative AI, including LLMs, will in the long term become unprofitable, and will be less integrated on the frontend, but in development and research will be more broadly used (at a cost), but in the even longer term, the costs will decrease, and it will be as broadly available as it is today, but even more useful and accurate.

Victoria: In its current form, I don't think it will be very relevant; it could persist in specific specialized uses where it's actually worthwhile, but I don't think the general-population generative AI will persist.

3. What would you want out of AI in the future? In other words, if you could shape the future of AI to cater to your needs, what would you do with it?

Bobby: It needs to be less convincing to vulnerable people (by which I mean people more easily influenced by misinformation, inaccurate claims, and hallucinations.

Victoria: Other than those specialized uses above, I want it to die out.

4. Do you think AI poses any relevant threats to the environment?

Bobby: To a certain extent, but things like agriculture, automotive transport, construction, and climate control (that is, in the home and workspace; A/C) are far, far more detrimental, and AI's benefits will likely outweigh its detriment.

Victoria: If it persists, yes; it's very wasteful of energy and doesn't even produce anything worthwhile for the majority of its use to justify this at all.

5. Do you think AI poses any relevant threats to people (outside of issues that stem from the environment)?

Bobby: Yes, but it's overstated, and we've always been able to adapt.

Victoria: Yes; it doesn't provide trustworthy information but some people are deluded into thinking it does, which can cause problems if they try to do something based on it.

6. What are some of the biggest environmental threats you see in the world right now, and how much of an issue do you think they will pose in the future?

Bobby: Increased frequency of extreme weather events, special migration/dehabitation/extinction,
invasive species. They'll be a bigger threat in the future, but not even close to human extinction,
or really even death on a large scale.

Victoria: Really just all the same stuff with global climate change, fossil fuels, etc.; how much of an issue it is really depends on if we start seriously doing something about it or not.

7. Right now, do you think we’re allocating too little, just enough, or too many resources to the development of AI? Explain your point of view.

Bobby: "We" as in the government, too little. "We" as in all people, just enough.

Victoria: Far too much.

8. With all positive and negative effects taken into account, do you think AI is a net benefit or negative for society?

Bobby: I'd say net positive, mais on ne sait jamais. TRANSLATION: “but you never know”

Victoria: Negative.

9. Do you often see or notice AI in parts of your everyday life that you didn’t expect it to appear? In other words, have you noticed AI appearing in places you never expected it too? (i.e. tech support or personal assistant AIs on certain websites, AI-generated imagery out in the wild, etc.)

Bobby: Yes

Victoria: Briefly, before I figure out the ways to block it.

10. If you could change one thing about AI (literally anything in terms of how it works, how it's presented, the back end of it, ethical implications, it’s role in society, etc.), what would you change about it and why?

Bobby: I'd make people stop using it to generate art, but also make the anti-ai crowd understand that ai-generated art isn't inherently unethical.

Victoria: I would change the fact that it exists.

we met Jared, a millennial manager, who does his best to create a happy and loving work culture for his team.
We were surprised to notice: that despite his decent knowledge about AI, he couldn’t care less about it, its development, or its consequences.
We wonder if this means that: AI doesn’t provide enough meaningful utility to affect certain job spaces or professions, such as Jared’s.
ItIt would be game-changing to: have AI be useful enough to help Jared in his goal of creating a
welcoming, but efficient, workplace for all.

POV 2:

we met Jessica, an aspiring software engineer with the goal of making the world a better place.
We were surprised to notice: that she is very hesitant about using AI due to the numerous
things she’s heard about its negative environmental impacts.
We wonder if this means that:, in Jessica's eyes, AI is a very powerful and appreciated tool,
simply locked behind a cost too great to justify using it.
It would be game-changing to: provide a way for Jessica to use AI tools without having any
negative repercussions on the environment.

POV 3:

we met Aryan, a happy-go-lucky person, who aspires to be a historian.
We were surprised to notice: that Aryan mentioned that he prefers using Google since he
doesn’t trust the information AI gives him.
We wonder if this means that: he will always view AI as useless, and ultimately subpar to other,
more verifiable forms of research and information-seeking.
It would be game-changing to: have AI tools be more reliable and consistent with things like
fact-checking, which would allow Aryan to place more faith in the information AI presents to him.

POV 4:

we met Jerry, a 50 year-old Facebook parent, who loves funny AI imagery and enjoys talking
about it with others online.
We were surprised to notice: that Jerry mentioned how he generates hundreds of images a day, asking what the harm was in having fun.
We wonder if this means Jerry has no knowledge about how AI generated content is made, or
any of its environmental issues.
It would be game-changing to: provide more transparency on the backend of AI and its
development, so that people know the risks before engaging with it.

We wonder if this means that: Jorge feels like putting AI tools in the classroom is unnecessary,

It would be game-changing to: provide a safe way to use AI so that Jorge could have
peace-of-mind.

POV 6:

we met Jennifer, an environmental activist set on making sure that stays happy, healthy, and
green.
We were surprised to notice: her complete and full-fledged disdain for all things relating to AI,
especially for the impact it has on the environment.
We wonder if this means that: Jennifer thinks that recent AI developments are daunting and
overly promoted, while the environmental downsides that come with AI are extremely scary and

It would be game-changing to: show Jennifer that AI tools have value and will continue to have

POV 7:

we met Gerald, a database manager who’s dead set on making AI systems as powerful and

We were surprised to notice: that Gerald became stressed out when the issue of

We wonder if this means that: Gerald, despite having deep knowledge on LLM development, is
unsure about how to make AI systems eco-friendly.
It would be game-changing if: there was some way on the backend to either reshape or
remove some of the environmental effects of AI.
